Round 1

Ideation & Evaluation

Creative PM

Family Knowledge Base — Feature Ideas


---


1. Family Profiles & Personas

Each family member gets a named profile with age, interests, and a "voice" preference (e.g., explain things simply vs. technically). Claude tailors every response to the active profile automatically.

User value: Conversations feel personal and age-appropriate without manual context-setting each time. Complexity: Medium

---


2. Tiered Privacy Vaults

KB entries are tagged with visibility tiers: Everyone, Adults Only, or Named Members. Parents set tiers from a simple admin view; kids' sessions silently skip restricted entries and Claude never hints they exist.

User value: Parents share sensitive documents (finances, medical, legal) without exposing them or creating awkward "you can't see that" moments. Complexity: Medium

---


3. Family Memory Timeline

A chronological feed of life events (birthdays, moves, school milestones, vacations) stored in the KB. Claude can surface relevant memories contextually — "That trip to Yosemite in 2019 might be worth mentioning here."

User value: Preserves family history and makes conversations richer and more emotionally resonant. Complexity: Medium

---


4. Document Digest Mode

Upload dense documents (insurance policies, wills, tax returns) and Claude auto-generates a plain-English summary stored alongside the original. Kids see only the summary; adults see both.

User value: Makes intimidating paperwork accessible and searchable without legal jargon. Complexity: Low

---


5. Shared Homework & Learning Hub

A KB section where kids can deposit school topics, essay drafts, and reading lists. Claude references this to give tutoring help that connects to their actual coursework — not generic examples.

User value: Homework help becomes curriculum-aware and continuous across sessions, not one-off. Complexity: Low

---


6. Family Recipe & Tradition Keeper

Store recipes, holiday rituals, and cultural traditions in structured KB entries. Claude can generate shopping lists, scale recipes for guests, and explain the story behind a tradition to curious kids.

User value: Keeps living family culture from dying in a drawer or a relative's head. Complexity: Low

---


7. Emergency Information Card

A high-priority KB entry with medical allergies, emergency contacts, insurance numbers, and care instructions. Accessible to all members but flagged so Claude surfaces it instantly when queries sound urgent.

User value: Critical information is always one question away, even for kids in a stressful moment. Complexity: Low

---


8. Parent Approval Queue

When a child's query touches a restricted topic or entry, it's logged for parents with a "unlock for this child" option. Parents can grant one-time or permanent access from a notification.

User value: Turns privacy controls into a conversation starter rather than a hard wall. Complexity: High

---


9. Knowledge Base Health Score

A dashboard showing KB completeness: missing emergency contacts, outdated financial summaries, unreviewed entries. Claude proactively nudges the family to fill gaps.

User value: Keeps the KB useful over time instead of becoming stale after initial setup. Complexity: Medium

---


10. Collaborative Entry Editing

Any family member can propose an edit or addition to a KB entry; it queues for a parent to approve before going live. Great for kids contributing memories or facts they know.

User value: The KB becomes a living family project, not just a parent-managed archive. Complexity: High

---

---


Fatal Flaws — Cut These


#8 — Parent Approval Queue

Fatal flaw: It manufactures a frustrating dead-end mid-conversation for children. A child asks something, gets no answer, and must wait for a parent notification that may arrive hours later. That's a product-killing experience. Worse, it creates notification fatigue for parents and a complex async state machine for engineers. The actual problem — keeping restricted content restricted — is already solved by #2. This idea is a solution to a problem that shouldn't exist in a well-designed privacy model.


#10 — Collaborative Entry Editing

Fatal flaw: Families are not Wikipedia editors. The "living family project" framing sounds compelling but mismaps the user's motivation. People want answers, not a workflow. An approval queue on top of KB edits compounds the friction of #8 without the same justification. High effort, low retention payoff, and it degrades content quality by inviting unreviewed contributions.


---


Ranking of Remaining 8 Ideas


1. #7 Emergency Information Card — Lowest effort, highest trust signal. Ships first, earns credibility.

2. #2 Tiered Privacy Vaults — Without this, parents never fully commit. It is the adoption gate.

3. #1 Family Profiles & Personas — Foundational infrastructure; every other feature is weaker without it.

4. #4 Document Digest Mode — Immediate, tangible value on first use. Low effort, high retention.

5. #5 Homework & Learning Hub — Drives daily engagement from the demographic (kids) most likely to use it repeatedly.

6. #9 KB Health Score — Prevents the product from becoming a graveyard after week two. Underrated retention lever.

7. #3 Family Memory Timeline — Emotionally differentiated, but only valuable once the KB has content mass. Build later.

8. #6 Recipe & Tradition Keeper — Cut or deprioritize. Paprika exists. The *tradition* angle is the only genuine differentiator, but it is too thin to justify a dedicated feature before the core is stable.


---


Key Risks Across the Set


  • LLM privacy leakage in #2: Claude can be prompted to reveal what it "knows" indirectly. The privacy vault requires aggressive system-prompt hardening and testing, not just access-control logic.
  • Profile switching in #1 is a security surface: If a child can switch to an adult profile, #2 collapses entirely. Auth must be tied to profiles.
  • #7 is deceptively simple but liability-adjacent: If emergency info is stale and someone relies on it, that is a trust catastrophe. Consider a mandatory "last verified" timestamp with expiry nudges.

---


Convergence Decision


The top five ideas (#7, #2, #1, #4, #5) are strong and form a coherent MVP. However, the list is not ready to move forward as-is because:


1. The interaction between #1 (profiles) and #2 (privacy tiers) has an unresolved authentication design question that will dictate the architecture of everything else.

2. #6 needs an explicit cut decision rather than a soft deprioritization — it will keep resurfacing in roadmap discussions otherwise.

3. #9 (Health Score) needs a scoping discussion: is this a product feature or an onboarding/email nudge? The answer changes the effort estimate significantly.

↻ Needs refinement — Resolve the profile/privacy authentication model first — it is the architectural keystone. Explicitly cut Recipe & Tradition Keeper or reframe it around a defensible differentiator. Scope KB Health Score as a notification vs. dashboard feature before committing to it.
Round 2

Ideation & Evaluation

Creative PM

Family Knowledge Base — Refined Feature Ideas (Round 2)


Incorporating the critical feedback: resolving the profile/privacy authentication question, making explicit cut decisions, and scoping the Health Score more precisely.


---


ARCHITECTURE RESOLUTION FIRST


1. Verified Profile Sessions *(replaces and merges #1 + #2)*

When a family member starts a session, they select their profile and authenticate with a short PIN (set by a parent for child profiles, self-set for adults). The PIN gates which privacy tier Claude operates within for the entire session — there is no mid-session switching. Parents can reset any PIN; children cannot escalate their own tier. Claude's system prompt is rebuilt per-session based on the authenticated tier, so restricted entries are never loaded into context at all — not suppressed, never present.


User value: Eliminates the security surface where a child switches to an adult profile, and removes the LLM-leakage risk from #2 by ensuring restricted content is architecturally absent rather than behaviorally hidden. Complexity: Medium (the PIN mechanism is simple; the per-session context scoping is the real engineering work)

---


CORE MVP (CONFIRMED, REFINED)


2. Emergency Information Card *(unchanged — ships first)*

A pinned, always-accessible KB entry containing medical allergies, emergency contacts, insurance numbers, and care instructions. Surfaces instantly when Claude detects urgency in a query. Includes a mandatory "last verified" date; after 90 days, Claude begins appending a gentle staleness warning to any emergency-adjacent response until a parent re-confirms.


User value: Critical information is one question away for any family member, including kids in a stressful moment — and the staleness mechanism prevents the liability scenario of someone trusting outdated data. Complexity: Low

---


3. Document Digest Mode *(unchanged)*

Upload dense documents — insurance policies, wills, tax returns — and Claude auto-generates a plain-English summary stored alongside the original. Privacy tier determines what each profile sees: children get only the summary; adults get both. Digests are re-generated if the source document is replaced.


User value: Intimidating paperwork becomes searchable and human-readable on first upload, without requiring the family to do any manual summarization work. Complexity: Low

---


4. Homework & Learning Hub *(unchanged)*

A KB section where children deposit active school topics, essay drafts, reading lists, and upcoming exams. Claude references this automatically when helping with schoolwork, connecting tutoring to actual curriculum rather than generic examples. Parents can view the section; children own it.


User value: Homework help becomes continuous and curriculum-aware across sessions, not a one-off interaction that forgets context the moment the tab closes. Complexity: Low

---


SCOPED & RESOLVED FEATURES


5. KB Health Nudges *(#9 scoped — notification, not dashboard)*

Rather than a dashboard (high effort, low visit frequency), Health Score becomes a weekly digest notification — email or in-app — that surfaces three specific gaps: the most outdated entry, the most-queried topic with no KB backing, and any Emergency Card fields that are empty or expired. Clicking any item deep-links directly to the fix. No dashboard to build or maintain.


User value: The KB stays useful over time without requiring parents to proactively audit it — the product does the remembering. Complexity: Low (notifications + a simple staleness query; no UI dashboard required)

---


6. Family Memory Timeline *(retained, gated on content mass)*

A chronological feed of life events — moves, births, school milestones, vacations — stored as lightweight KB entries. Claude surfaces relevant memories contextually when they genuinely enrich a response. Designed as a secondary onboarding path: the app prompts the family to add five founding memories during setup, then grows organically.


User value: Preserves family history in a form Claude can actively use, making conversations emotionally resonant rather than purely transactional. Complexity: Medium *(build after core is stable — only valuable once KB has content mass)*

---


EXPLICIT CUT & REFRAME


~~Recipe & Tradition Keeper~~ → Cultural Traditions Archive *(reframed or cut)*

The recipe angle is indefensible against Paprika and a dozen competitors. The only differentiator worth building is the tradition layer: the *why* and *story* behind a family ritual, not the recipe itself. A stripped-down version — a simple structured entry type for "Tradition Name / Origin Story / How We Do It / Who Remembers It" — costs almost nothing to build and is genuinely unaddressed by any other tool. Claude can retell these stories to children in age-appropriate language on demand.


Decision point for stakeholders: If we cannot staff even a low-complexity feature without crowding out #5 or #3, cut this entirely. The tradition archive is a delight feature, not a retention driver. Do not let it slip into the roadmap without an explicit "yes" — it will keep resurfacing otherwise.
Complexity: Low *(if scoped to traditions only, no recipe functionality)*

---


NEW IDEA: Closing a Gap the Previous Round Missed


7. Life Event Prompts

When a family member mentions a significant event in conversation — a new job, a diagnosis, a college acceptance — Claude flags it and offers to create a KB entry or update an existing one on the spot. "It sounds like something important just happened. Want me to save this to your family's timeline?" Parents approve additions from kids; adults self-approve.


User value: Captures life context in the moment rather than requiring families to maintain the KB as a separate chore — the KB grows as a byproduct of normal conversation. Complexity: Medium *(requires intent detection and a lightweight in-conversation approval flow)*

---


Convergence Status


The authentication model is now resolved (Verified Profile Sessions). Recipe & Tradition Keeper has an explicit decision gate. Health Score is scoped to notifications. The MVP is:


Emergency Card → Verified Profile Sessions → Document Digest → Homework Hub → Health Nudges

That is a coherent, shippable sequence with no unresolved architectural dependencies.

Fatal Flaws — Elimination Review


No full eliminations, but two features carry conditional red flags:
Feature 1 — Verified Profile Sessions: The spec says "Claude's system prompt is rebuilt per-session based on the authenticated tier." This is architecturally correct in principle, but it sidesteps the harder question: what happens when the *same physical device* is used by multiple family members without a session handoff? If there's no session timeout or explicit logout, PIN-gating is security theater — a child simply inherits the prior adult session. This is not a reason to cut, but it must be resolved before any security claim is made in marketing or onboarding copy. Conditional flag: session expiry policy is missing.
Feature 7 — Life Event Prompts: Intent detection at the quality threshold needed here is harder than stated. "A new job, a diagnosis, a college acceptance" — these are easy examples. Real conversations are ambiguous. False positives (Claude interrupting routine conversation to offer KB saves) will erode trust faster than false negatives. The approval flow also introduces latency into what should feel like a natural conversation. This is not fatal but the "Medium complexity" label is optimistic. Conditional flag: false positive rate needs a design bound before build begins.

---


Ranked List (by combined value/risk ratio)


1. Emergency Information Card — Unambiguous value, lowest risk, highest urgency. Ships first, full stop.

2. Verified Profile Sessions — Not optional; everything downstream depends on this. Must ship concurrent with or immediately after Emergency Card.

3. KB Health Nudges — Underrated. This is the feature that makes the KB *not die* six months post-launch. Notification-first scoping is correct and protects retention.

4. Document Digest Mode — Concrete, differentiable in a family context, clean scope. Third in sequence.

5. Homework & Learning Hub — Solid. Narrow audience somewhat; sequence after Digest to avoid parallel build complexity.

6. Life Event Prompts — High potential upside for KB growth flywheel, but build *after* the KB has enough structure to receive entries gracefully. Premature without features 2–5 in place.

7. Family Memory Timeline — Correctly back-burnered. Only valuable with content mass. Do not build until retention data from core features justifies it.

8. Cultural Traditions Archive — The explicit decision gate framing is exactly right. Do not let this drift onto the roadmap. Revisit in a dedicated session with staffing context in hand; otherwise treat as cut.


---


Convergence Assessment


This set is materially stronger than Round 1. The authentication resolution removes the biggest architectural ambiguity. Health Score descoping from dashboard to notification is the right call and shows genuine prioritization discipline. The explicit decision gate on Traditions is good product hygiene.


Two items still need tightening before the plan is truly locked:

1. Session expiry / handoff policy for Verified Profile Sessions must be specified — even one sentence in the spec.

2. Life Event Prompts needs a false-positive design constraint (e.g., "only trigger if confidence > X" or "only trigger on explicit user statement, not inference") before it enters any sprint.


These are spec gaps, not reasons to run another full ideation round. The feature set itself is converged.
