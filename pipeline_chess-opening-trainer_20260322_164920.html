Creative PM

Chess Opening Trainer — Feature Ideas


---


1. Opening DNA Profile

Generate a visual "fingerprint" of a player's opening repertoire based on their game history (imported via PGN or connected to Lichess/Chess.com). Shows which openings they gravitate toward, their win rates, and blind spots.

User value: Players understand their own style and get personalized recommendations rather than generic content. Complexity: Medium

---


2. Mistake Replay Arena

After a training session, the trainer surfaces the 3 moves you got wrong most often and drills *only* those positions — spaced-repetition style — until you nail them consistently.

User value: Eliminates the frustration of re-studying what you already know; focuses effort where it matters. Complexity: Low

---


3. Grandmaster Storytelling Mode

Each opening comes with a narrative: the historical duel where it was first played, the player's psychology, and why the move order matters. Moves are revealed cinematic-ally with annotations voiced in character.

User value: Makes memorization emotionally sticky; learners remember stories, not move strings. Complexity: Medium

---


4. Opponent Simulator

Train against a simulated opponent who specifically *exploits* your repertoire gaps. The engine knows which side-lines you haven't studied and steers into them.

User value: Prepares players for real over-the-board surprises; closes the gap between study and practice. Complexity: High

---


5. Opening Clash — Async Multiplayer

Challenge a friend to a "repertoire duel": both players are quizzed on the same opening tree and scores are compared on a shared leaderboard. Trash-talk thread included.

User value: Social accountability and friendly competition dramatically increase return visits and study consistency. Complexity: Medium

---


6. Move Intention Tagging

Before revealing the correct move, the player picks the *idea* behind it (e.g., "Control center," "Develop minor piece," "Provoke weakness"). Right move, wrong idea = partial credit and a short lesson.

User value: Builds positional understanding, not just pattern matching — the difference between a good amateur and a strong club player. Complexity: Low

---


7. Live Over-the-Board Import

After an OTB tournament game, scan or enter the opening moves; the trainer immediately shows where you deviated from your prep and what the engine preferred.

User value: Closes the feedback loop between study and real games instantly, while the game is fresh. Complexity: Medium
Critical PM

Critical PM Evaluation — Chess Opening Trainer


---


Scoring


| # | Idea | Feasibility | User Impact | Effort | Notes |

|---|------|-------------|-------------|--------|-------|

| 1 | Opening DNA Profile | 7 | 8 | 6 | API integrations + visualization; strong retention hook |

| 2 | Mistake Replay Arena | 9 | 9 | 3 | Spaced repetition is solved; this is the core learning loop |

| 3 | Grandmaster Storytelling | 4 | 6 | 9 | See fatal flaw below |

| 4 | Opponent Simulator | 5 | 9 | 9 | High reward, high risk; knowledge modeling is hard to get right |

| 5 | Opening Clash | 6 | 6 | 7 | Social infrastructure cost outweighs marginal retention gain |

| 6 | Move Intention Tagging | 9 | 8 | 3 | Low effort, high pedagogical differentiation |

| 7 | Live OTB Import | 7 | 8 | 5 | Closes the study-play loop; clear use case for tournament players |


---


Fatal Flaws


#3 — Grandmaster Storytelling Mode: ELIMINATED

This is a content business disguised as a software feature. To do it properly, you need historically accurate narratives for hundreds of opening variations — written, fact-checked, and voiced. That is an ongoing operational cost that compounds with every new opening added. If you substitute AI-generated narration, you risk factual errors on historical games (a credibility-killer in the chess community, which is intensely pedantic). The "cinematic" voiceover is a production pipeline, not a product feature. Kill it unless content is a declared core competency.


---


Concerns (not fatal, but real)


#4 — Opponent Simulator: The user knowledge model is the entire bet. If the gap-detection is wrong, the sim steers users into lines they *have* studied, or overwhelms them with lines that are too far outside their prep. Either outcome is frustrating, not educational. This needs a very tight prototype before committing to full build.
#5 — Opening Clash: The "trash-talk thread" is a product smell — it signals the social angle needs justification. Chess opening study skews solo and intrinsic. Async multiplayer demands account infrastructure, notifications, and moderation. The feature needs a sharper thesis: is the goal accountability (study streaks with a friend) or competition (leaderboards)? As spec'd, it's neither cleanly.

---


Final Rankings


1. #2 Mistake Replay Arena — Build first. Lowest effort, highest impact, directly solves the #1 training frustration. This is the retention engine.

2. #6 Move Intention Tagging — Build alongside #2. Cheap, differentiating, and pedagogically superior to every competitor doing rote drilling. This is your positioning.

3. #1 Opening DNA Profile — Strong second release. Personalization hooks users and justifies continued engagement. Requires platform integrations but the value is clear.

4. #7 Live OTB Import — High value for the serious segment. Medium effort. Strong for tournament season marketing.

5. #4 Opponent Simulator — Prototype only. Validate the gap-detection accuracy before committing. If the model is good, this becomes your flagship. If not, it's a frustrating product.

6. #5 Opening Clash — Deprioritize. Rewrite the brief to pick one of: accountability (friend streaks) or competition (leaderboards). Don't build both until one proves out.


---


Convergence Verdict


The top four ideas (#2, #6, #1, #7) form a coherent, sequenced roadmap with clear user value and manageable scope. #3 is cleanly cut. #4 and #5 need sharper specs before prioritization. The remaining set is actionable.
